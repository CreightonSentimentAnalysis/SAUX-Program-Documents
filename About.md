This research explores the use of LLMs for providing quantitative zero-shot sentiment analysis of implicit software desirability, 
addressing a critical challenge in product evaluation where traditional review scores fail to capture the richness of qualitative user feedback. 
There are several key innovations from this search, and several lines of current research that are being explored.

Innovations include: 
1) Establishing a method that provides both numerical quantitative sentiment as well as categorical sentiment, using qualitative user experience data without the need for explicit review scores,
2) Improving the quality implicit user satisfaction methods,
3) Providing scaled numerical sentiment analysis, offering a nuanced understanding of user sentiment,
   along with classifying sentiment into 3 categories (positive, neutral, or negative) or classifying sentiment into 5 categories (very positive,positive, neutral, negative, or very negative),

4) This research also explores trade-offs between cost and performance in LLMs by comparing LLMs such as GPT-4o with their much smaller and more cost effective alternatives, such as GPT-4o-mini.
5) It also explores usig bagging or ensembles of low cost methods for improved results
6) Explores aspects of user sentiment
7) Explores multi-modal sentiment data for refined analysis
8) Explores explainable AI
9) Primarily uses data from the Microsoft Product Desirability Toolkit (PDT) is used and analyzed by the models, with performance measured via sentiment alignment, textual diversity, and data generation cost.  Our work shows that GPT-4o-mini achieved statistically significant sentiment scoring while maintaining a significantly lower computational cost than GPT-4o.
10) Using the PDT survey and data with LLM analysis, creating marketing messages and product improvement suggestions
11) explores other sources of user experience data
